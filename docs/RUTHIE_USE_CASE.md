# Ruthie's Week: A Concrete Use Case

*One real child. One real week. What mom used to do manually versus what MyAIM does instead.*

---

## Who Ruthie Is

Ruthie is seven years old. She has Down Syndrome. She lives with her mom, dad, and eight siblings in Missouri. She participates in the state's Self-Directed Services (SDS) program — a Medicaid waiver that allows her family to hire and manage their own support staff. She has two aides: Amy and Kylie.

Ruthie's story isn't hypothetical. She is the founder's daughter. Every schedule, every report, every coordination challenge described below is drawn from real life. MyAIM was designed around her needs because no existing tool could hold the weight of what her family carries.

## Tuesday: Before MyAIM

**6:30 AM** — Mom wakes up. Mentally reviews today: co-op at 9 (Kylie is the aide), OT at 2, speech at 3. Checks phone for text from Kylie confirming she's coming. No text yet. Sends a reminder text.

**8:45 AM** — Gets Ruthie ready. Kylie arrives. Mom verbally tells Kylie what happened yesterday: "Ruthie had a rough afternoon, lots of hand-flapping, seemed overstimulated. Watch for that today." Kylie nods. None of this is written down.

**12:15 PM** — Kylie texts: "Good day! She shared toys with another kid at snack time." Mom sees the text between making lunch for four other kids. Makes a mental note to include the sharing milestone in the monthly report. Forgets by 3 PM.

**2:00 PM** — OT therapy. Mom drives Ruthie. Therapist asks about progress on fine motor goals. Mom tries to remember specifics from the last two weeks. Gives an approximation. Doesn't have data.

**3:00 PM** — Speech therapy. Similar conversation. Mom knows Ruthie has been using more two-word phrases but can't remember when it started or how consistently.

**9:00 PM** — Kids in bed. Mom opens a Google Doc to start updating the monthly SDS report. Scrolls through text messages from Kylie and Amy over the past month. Cross-references with a paper calendar. Tries to reconstruct what happened on which days. This takes 45 minutes before she even starts writing. She's exhausted. She'll finish tomorrow. Or the next day.

**Total time lost to coordination and documentation: ~2.5 hours across the day** (scattered across texts, mental tracking, verbal handoffs, and evening report work).

---

## Tuesday: With MyAIM

**6:30 AM** — Mom opens MyAIM. Dashboard shows today's schedule: co-op (Kylie, 9 AM), OT (2 PM), speech (3 PM). Kylie's shift is auto-confirmed through the scheduling system.

**8:45 AM** — Kylie opens her Special Adult shell on her phone. Her shift starts. She can see Ruthie's recent entries — including yesterday's overstimulation note that mom logged in 15 seconds through a voice entry: "Rough afternoon, lots of hand-flapping, seemed overstimulated after the grocery store." Kylie knows what to watch for without a verbal handoff.

**12:15 PM** — Kylie logs a shift note directly in MyAIM: "Great co-op day. Shared toys with another kid at snack time — first time without prompting. Hand-flapping was minimal." This is timestamped, attributed, and automatically tagged as progress toward Ruthie's social interaction ISP goal.

**2:00 PM** — OT therapy. Mom pulls up Ruthie's recent widget data on her phone. Shows the therapist a two-week trend of fine motor tracking entries. The therapist sees specific data, not approximations.

**3:00 PM** — Speech therapy. Mom shows the speech therapist that Ruthie's two-word phrase frequency increased 40% over the past month — captured automatically through daily log entries.

**9:00 PM** — Kids in bed. Mom doesn't open a Google Doc. The monthly SDS report isn't due for two weeks, but when it is, LiLa will gather all of Kylie's shift notes, Amy's shift notes, mom's daily observations, behavioral tracking widget data, and goal progress — then ask mom 3-5 follow-up questions and generate the complete report. Mom reviews, edits if needed, approves. Done in 15 minutes.

**Total time lost to coordination and documentation: ~10 minutes** (a voice note, a glance at the dashboard, showing the phone to two therapists).

## The Monthly SDS Report: Before vs. After

### Before MyAIM
1. Scroll through a month of text messages from two aides
2. Cross-reference with paper calendar and Google Calendar
3. Try to reconstruct what happened on which days
4. Review behavioral notes (if they exist — often they don't)
5. Write narrative prose for each ISP goal area
6. Write health update section from memory
7. Calculate budget utilization from separate spreadsheet
8. Format for the Acumen DCI compliance portal
9. Proofread, second-guess, submit

**Time: 3-4 hours. Emotional cost: significant.** This task hangs over the entire last week of every month.

### With MyAIM
1. Click "Generate SDS Monthly Summary"
2. LiLa gathers: all aide shift logs, all daily observations, all behavioral tracking data, all goal progress, budget data
3. LiLa shows what it found: "From the shift notes, it looks like Ruthie went to co-op on Tuesdays and Thursdays, had activity days with Amy, and Kylie noted improvements in sharing. Does that sound right?"
4. Mom answers 3-5 targeted follow-up questions (health updates, staffing changes, anything to add)
5. LiLa generates the full report in mom's voice — warm, personal, detailed, compliant
6. Mom reviews through Human-in-the-Mix: Edit / Approve / Regenerate / Reject
7. Final output: copy-paste ready for the compliance portal + saved as PDF in Archives

**Time: 15-20 minutes. Emotional cost: minimal.** The data was captured throughout the month as part of normal daily life, not reconstructed from memory under deadline pressure.
