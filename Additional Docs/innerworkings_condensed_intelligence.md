## What This Document Is

InnerWorkings is the personal self-knowledge profile system for all family members in MyAIM Family. It stores what each person knows about themselves — personality, strengths, processing style, love languages, values, and more — so that LiLa (the AI) can provide deeply personalized support across every other tool.

Three input paths: (1) guided discovery conversations with LiLa, (2) manual entry by the user, (3) upload/extract from assessments, test results, or personal documents.

This document is the distilled intelligence from a large extraction of books used to build InnerWorkings. Each item includes the source book in italics.

## Section 1: Personality Type & Trait Discovery

*Frameworks for helping users identify their core personality type and characteristic patterns. These become the foundational layer of the InnerWorkings profile.*

- **Enneagram as Master Filter** — The Enneagram type functions as a "master filter" through which all of life is perceived. Each type has a Basic Fear (what it most wants to avoid) and a Basic Desire (what it most wants to feel or become) — these are the deep motivational drivers, not just surface behaviors. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Basic Fear/Desire Pairs by Type** — Type 1: fear of being wrong/corrupt, desire to have integrity. Type 2: fear of being unloved, desire to feel loved. Type 3: fear of being worthless, desire to feel valuable. Type 4: fear of being without identity, desire to be significant. Type 5: fear of being useless, desire to be competent. Type 6: fear of being without support, desire to feel secure. Type 7: fear of being deprived, desire to be satisfied. Type 8: fear of being controlled, desire to protect themselves. Type 9: fear of loss/separation, desire to have inner stability. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Lost Childhood Messages by Type** — Each type formed around a message they didn't adequately receive as a child: Type 1: "You are good." Type 2: "You are wanted." Type 3: "You are loved for yourself." Type 4: "You are seen for who you are." Type 5: "Your needs are not a problem." Type 6: "You are safe." Type 7: "You will be taken care of." Type 8: "You will not be betrayed." Type 9: "Your presence matters." These messages, offered sincerely, touch the deepest healing point for each type. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **The Nine Passions (Core Emotional Drives)** — Each type has a characteristic emotional passion that drives much of their behavior: anger (Type 1), pride (Type 2), deceit/self-deception (Type 3), envy (Type 4), avarice/hoarding (Type 5), fear (Type 6), gluttony/excess (Type 7), lust/intensity (Type 8), sloth/self-forgetting (Type 9). These are not sins but habitual coping responses. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Harmonic Groups** — Three clusters that reveal how each type copes with conflict and difficulty: Positive Outlook Group (Types 2, 7, 9 — reframe negative to positive, minimize difficulties), Competency Group (Types 1, 3, 5 — solve problems objectively, suppress feelings), Reactive Group (Types 4, 6, 8 — have strong emotional reactions, need to express feelings before they can work on problems). *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Levels of Development** — Each type exists on a spectrum from unhealthy (Levels 7-9, destructive behaviors) through average (Levels 4-6, characteristic coping patterns) to healthy (Levels 1-3, the type's gifts fully expressed). The same person can move up or down this scale within a day based on stress, sleep, and felt security. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Wake-Up Calls** — Each type has a characteristic early warning sign that they are beginning to lose healthy functioning: Type 1: feeling personally responsible for fixing everything; Type 2: believing they must give more to be loved; Type 3: driving to perform and impress; Type 4: holding on to and intensifying feelings; Type 5: withdrawing and hoarding resources; Type 6: feeling increased anxiety and apprehension; Type 7: seeking more stimulation and options; Type 8: feeling that they must control their environment; Type 9: habitually numbing themselves. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Type 1 — The Reformer** — Driven by a need for integrity, correctness, and moral purpose. Core gift: incorruptible discernment. Core trap: perfectionism where the goalposts auto-elevate, making satisfaction impossible. The inner critic sounds authoritative but is not the voice of truth — it creates internal war, not guidance. Moves toward Four under stress (moody, melancholy) and toward Seven in integration (spontaneous, joyful). *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Type 2 — The Helper** — Warm, empathetic, generous — but gives in order to get. The real need beneath all the giving is not returned favors but unconditional love. Core trap: self-abandonment while ministering to everyone else. Healing begins when the Two admits "I have needs and I am hurting." Moves toward Eight under stress (surprisingly forceful and blunt) and toward Four in integration (genuine self-knowledge and emotional honesty). *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Type 3 — The Achiever** — Success-oriented, image-conscious, adaptable. Core trap: substituting image for identity and performance for presence. The most dangerous form of their characteristic deceit is self-deception — convincing themselves they are the idealized image they project. "Human doings instead of human beings." Healing: Essential value (I am valuable simply because I exist). *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Type 4 — The Individualist** — Deeply attuned to emotion, beauty, and inner life. Core trap: building identity around emotional states and attaching to longing itself. The Sufi dog and the bone: the Four holds onto suffering as the only identity they know, unable to receive the abundance already present. Envy is not of things but of the seeming completeness others possess. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Type 5 — The Investigator** — Visionary thinker who collects knowledge as preparation for life. Core trap: perpetual preparation mode — gathering information instead of living. The Inner Tinker Toy: a dominant internal structure that becomes a substitute for actual experience. Healing: no amount of preparation resolves the underlying anxiety — only actual engagement with life does. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **BEI Six Brain Poles (Cerebral Multipolarity)** — A framework beyond left-brain/right-brain: six distinct poles, each a different "window" on reality. NW = Analytical/Factual (data, proof, logic). NE = Intuitive/Conceptual (imagination, metaphors, big picture). SW = Organizational/Preventive (systems, sequences, structure). SE = Emotional/Relational (empathy, kinesthetic, people-focus). N = Metaphysical/Spiritual. S = Corporal/Visceral (body-based, instinctual). Most people are bi-dominant (strongest in two adjacent poles). *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*

- **Brain Pole Communication Compatibility** — People in the same pole communicate easily; neighboring poles in the same hemisphere face moderate difficulty; neighboring poles in opposite hemispheres encounter greater challenges; diametrically opposite poles experience extreme difficulty because they have conflicting interests, viewpoints, and representation systems. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*

- **Instinctual Variants (Enneagram)** — Three subtypes that shape HOW the type's patterns express: Self-Preservation (focused on physical security, health, comfort), Social (focused on belonging, group dynamics, status within communities), Sexual/Intimate (focused on intense one-on-one connections, chemistry, vitality). The dominant variant is an energy that colors every interaction. *(The Wisdom of the Enneagram)*

- **Big Five Personality Traits** — Five stable dimensions: Openness (curiosity vs. routine preference), Conscientiousness (organized/disciplined vs. flexible), Extraversion (outward vs. inward energy), Agreeableness (cooperative vs. skeptical), Neuroticism/Emotional Stability (reactive vs. steady). Unlike Enneagram, these measure magnitude not type — a spectrum on each dimension. *(Semantic Search)*

- **Talent Formula** — Talent × Investment = Strength. Natural talent without development produces potential, not results. Focused investment in non-talent areas produces limited returns regardless of effort. The highest-leverage question: what feels like play to me but looks like work to others? *(StrengthsFinder 2.0)*

- **Eight Clues to Natural Talent** — Yearning (drawn to certain activities in childhood before socialization), Rapid Learning (picking up a new skill faster than others), Flow (losing track of time during an activity), Glimpses of Excellence (unplanned moments of outstanding performance), Satisfaction (deep fulfillment from doing the activity, not just completing it), Voice (something you can talk about with authority and enthusiasm), Absorption (so focused you lose awareness of surroundings), Ease (what drains others replenishes you). *(StrengthsFinder 2.0)*

## Section 3: Growth Areas (Strength-Based Framing)

*How to surface and frame growth areas without shame. Never label these "weaknesses" in any UI element.*

- **"Growth Areas," Never "Weaknesses"** — This is a non-negotiable UI rule. The label "weakness" activates shame and defensiveness; "growth area" activates curiosity and agency. Applies to every surface in the product. *(StewardShip Design Principle)*

- **Fixed vs. Growth Mindset — Four Dimensions** — (1) Challenges: Fixed avoids; Growth embraces. (2) Obstacles: Fixed gives up; Growth persists. (3) Effort: Fixed sees as pointless for the talented; Growth sees as the path to mastery. (4) Criticism: Fixed ignores; Growth learns from. The same person can have a growth mindset in one domain and a fixed mindset in another — domain specificity matters. *(Mindset — Dweck)*

- **Attentional Filter and Mindset** — Brain wave research shows that people with a growth mindset have a different attentional filter than those with a fixed mindset — they pay more attention to mistakes, go deeper into feedback, and process learning at a neural level that produces actual change. *(Mindset — Dweck)*

- **"Yet" as a Mindset Shifter** — One of the highest-leverage growth interventions for teens: replacing "I can't do this" with "I can't do this yet." The single word opens a door from fixed endpoint to ongoing process. *(Growth Mindset for Teens)*

- **ABCDE Method for Growth** — Adversity → Belief (what I tell myself) → Consequences (how I feel and act) → Disputation (challenging the belief) → Energization (what happens after disputing). The key insight: beliefs about adversity, not adversity itself, determine emotional outcomes. *(Growth Mindset for Teens)*

- **Comfort Zone → Growth Zone** — Three zones: Comfort (familiar, low growth, can feel safe but stagnates), Growth (stretch, learning happening, optimal challenge level), Panic (overwhelmed, learning shuts down). InnerWorkings data helps LiLa identify which zone a person is operating in for different life areas. *(Growth Mindset for Teens)*

- **Neuroplasticity — Three Types** — Synaptic (strengthening existing connections through practice), Structural (brain regions physically grow with sustained use), Functional (different brain regions take over functions previously handled elsewhere). All three support the message: effort changes the brain itself, not just the skill. *(Growth Mindset for Teens)*

- **Managing Weaknesses — Three Paths** — Avoid the area if possible; build systems or structures to manage around it; partner with someone who has strength there. The goal is not eliminating awareness of a lesser talent but preventing it from becoming a fatal blind spot. *(StrengthsFinder 2.0)*

- **Curiosity as the Entry Point** — Contradictory entries in a self-profile are not errors. People are complex. The AI should note nuance rather than flag contradiction: "You've described yourself as patient but quick to anger when disrespected. That's a useful distinction." *(StewardShip Design Convention)*

- **Self-Compassion as Foundation for Growth** — Kristin Neff's three-component model: (1) Notice — acknowledge the difficult feeling without suppressing or dramatizing it. (2) Change — recognize this is a moment of suffering, part of common humanity. (3) Apply the friend-standard — what would I say to a close friend who shared this struggle? Self-compassion is not self-indulgence; it is the prerequisite for honest self-assessment. *(Multiple sources)*

- **Inner Critic as Protective Mechanism** — The harsh inner voice is not the voice of truth; it is an ego structure designed to keep the person safe from perceived dangers (rejection, failure, humiliation). Naming it as "the inner critic speaking" creates distance from it. *(IFS/Schwartz)*

## Section 4: Processing Style & Learning Preferences

*How people receive, organize, and express information — essential for LiLa to adapt its communication style and for helping family members understand each other.*

- **Preferred vs. Effective Learning Style** — A person's preferred style is not necessarily their most effective style. Just as preferring to exercise on a couch doesn't make it the best workout method, learning preferences don't always align with learning effectiveness. *(The Art of Raising Children for Greatness)*

- **Active vs. Passive Engagement** — The more active the engagement, the more effective the learning. This is the principle governing all retention and application — more reliable than any style category. *(The Art of Raising Children for Greatness)*

- **NLP Representation Systems Mapped to Brain Poles** — The four NLP systems reflect underlying brain dominances: Analytical/NW types prefer Digital (data, facts, proof). Organizational/SW types prefer Visual (spatial organization, maps, diagrams). Emotional/SE types prefer Kinesthetic (feeling-based, hands-on, proximity-needing). Intuitive/NE types prefer Auditory (spoken ideas, metaphors, speculation). *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*

- **Identifying Visual vs. Verbal Learners in Children** — Simple diagnostic: ask if they'd prefer to follow a drawing/map or read numbered directions to get from point A to point B. Test with Lego assembly: picture or written instructions? This quickly surfaces the core processing preference. *(Absolutely Organize Your Family)*

- **Kinesthetic Learners in Traditional Settings** — Kinesthetic learners require interactive engagement and proximity. When placed in traditional classroom settings (especially back rows), they experience discomfort and seek interaction, often mislabeled as undisciplined. Isolation from social interaction can neutralize their ability to learn. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*

- **Analytical/NW Learners** — Build knowledge architecturally through geometric assessment of facts and data. Require demonstrations, tests, and practical experiences. Metaphors and emotion-laden information have low credibility. Can only speculate through practical experience, testing hypotheses one by one. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*

- **Intuitive/NE Learners** — Process information holistically and speculatively, capturing ideas through metaphorical images. When they encounter worthy information, they interrupt reading to "walk their imagination" around the concept. Live instruction can be deficient because continuation interrupts their processing. Excel as self-taught learners. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*

- **Emotional/SE Learners** — Learn through relationship, warmth, and felt connection. Need music, personal items, comfortable environment, and relational context to learn effectively. Remove the relational element and their capacity diminishes. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*

- **Organizational/SW Learners** — Need clean lines, good lighting, reference materials, structured sequences, and clear systems. Respond to organized spaces, sequential background music, and step-by-step information. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*

- **Individual Learning Profile Components** — For thorough self-knowledge documentation: preferred learning style, emotional triggers, areas of strength, growth edges, optimal environment conditions, processing speed preference. Should be reviewed and updated as the person develops. *(The Learning Zone)*

- **Cognitive Diversity as Asset, Not Problem** — People are wired very differently due to genetics and brain structure. These differences are neither strengths nor weaknesses — they are simply different ways of operating mentally. The highest-performing teams and families are those that leverage this diversity rather than trying to homogenize it. *(Principles — Ray Dalio)*

## Section 5: Communication Style & Love Languages

*The two interpersonal dimensions that most directly feed other LiLa tools — especially Cyrano, Higgins, and relationship guidance.*

### Communication Style

- **Four Unhealthy Communication Styles** — Dove (conflict-avoiding, says yes when meaning no), Hawk (aggressive, uses intimidation), Owl (passive-aggressive, indirect), Ostrich (withdraws, stonewalls). Each is a coping strategy developed under relational stress, not a fixed identity. Identifying the default pattern is the first step toward change. *(Now You're Speaking My Language)*

- **Initiator Bears Responsibility for Adapting** — The person who initiates communication bears the primary responsibility for adapting to the other person's preferred style. This is a foundational communication principle that transforms how we frame "communication problems" in families. *(Now You're Speaking My Language)*

- **Emotional Contagion** — Emotions spread through social networks at measurable rates. Seeing angry content, spending time with a negative person, or texting with someone in distress measurably shifts your mood — often without noticing. Your social and digital environment is a mood environment, not just an information environment. *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*

### Love Languages

- **The Five Love Languages** — Words of Affirmation, Quality Time, Receiving Gifts, Acts of Service, Physical Touch. Each person has a primary language that fills their emotional "love tank" most efficiently. Understanding the difference between how you naturally express love and how your partner/child needs to receive it is the central application. *(The 5 Love Languages — Chapman)*

- **Three-Question Diagnostic for Adults** — (1) What does your partner/person do that hurts you most? (This often reveals, in reverse, their primary language.) (2) What do they most request of you? (They're asking you to speak their language.) (3) How do they typically express love? (People tend to give what they most want to receive.) *(The 5 Love Languages — Chapman)*

- **The Five-Clue Detective Approach for Children** — Observe: (1) How does the child express love to others? (2) What do they most frequently request? (3) What do they complain about? (4) What brings the highest emotional response when given? (5) How do they show love to their parents? *(The 5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*

- **Developmental Stage Calibration** — The way love languages are expressed must be calibrated not to chronological age but to developmental stage. A 16-year-old at a 10-year-old developmental stage receives Physical Touch more like a 10-year-old than a 16-year-old. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*

- **15-Week Observation Experiment** — For uncertain cases: three weeks each of concentrated focus on each language (skipping the one you're already doing). Observe behavior changes. The child who most "fills up" during a particular three-week period is likely showing their primary language. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*

- **Love Language Detection for Nonverbal/Special Needs Children** — Three-question approach: (1) What calms them when distressed? (2) What motivates them? (3) Where do they spend their time and energy? Behavioral indicators matter as much as verbal ones. Can also integrate IEP/504 plan knowledge for context. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*

- **All Five Languages Matter** — While the primary love language is most efficient at filling the emotional tank, all five languages contribute to a person's felt security and love. A person may prefer Acts of Service, but still needs affirmation, quality time, thoughtful gifts, and physical affection. Overspecializing on one language leads to imbalance. *(The 5 Love Languages — Chapman)*

- **Spouse vs. User Love Language** — For relationship coaching purposes: the user's own love language informs what they crave; the partner's love language informs what they need to receive. LiLa bridges this gap. Relationship suggestions prioritize the receiver's language, not the giver's natural expression. *(StewardShip Design Convention)*

## Section 6: Values, Faith & Spiritual Identity

*The meaning-making layer of the InnerWorkings profile — what a person lives by and what gives their life direction.*

- **Principles vs. Values Distinction (Covey)** — Principles are universal truths (fairness, integrity, human dignity) that exist independent of human agreement. Values are personal beliefs about what matters. A person can violate their own values without abandoning a principle. The Enneagram type often reveals which principles a person is wired to uphold most fiercely. *(7 Habits)*

- **Values Hierarchy Revealed Under Pressure** — What a person actually values most becomes visible when two values conflict under pressure. Self-reported values and lived values often diverge. The truest value clarification happens through reflection on past decisions made under constraint. *(7 Habits)*

- **Seven-Step Values Clarification** — (1) Prizing and cherishing the belief. (2) Affirming it publicly when challenged. (3) Choosing it freely from alternatives. (4) Choosing it after considering consequences. (5) Acting on it. (6) Acting repeatedly and consistently. (7) Creating a pattern of life that reflects it. Only what passes all seven tests is a true value. *(Semantic Search)*

- **Faith Identity as Journey, Not Destination** — Spiritual identity includes questions, doubts, evolving beliefs, and periods of re-examination as legitimate features, not failures. Asking hard questions is part of faith development, not evidence against it. An InnerWorkings profile should hold this complexity without forcing certainty. *(Semantic Search)*

- **Spiritual Identity Inventory Questions** — What do I believe about the nature of reality? What is the source of meaning in my life? What is my relationship with God or Ultimate Reality? What practices sustain my spiritual life? What do I believe about suffering, death, and what comes after? How does my faith community shape my identity? These questions open space for honest self-reflection. *(Semantic Search)*

- **Identity Anchored to Heavenly vs. Earthly Sources** — When identity is primarily anchored to role (parent, employee, spouse), performance (success, achievement), or approval (what others think), it becomes fragile. Identity anchored to spiritual source (inherent worth, belovedness, divine calling) provides stability when external circumstances shift. *(Semantic Search)*

- **Divine Center (Covey)** — When one's center is placed in eternal/divine principles rather than in spouse, work, money, possessions, pleasure, friends, enemies, church, or self, a unique security emerges. This center is not dependent on external circumstances and provides a stable foundation for all decision-making. *(7 Habits)*

## Section 7: Emotional Triggers, Sensitivities & Regulation

*Data that helps LiLa calibrate tone, pacing, and approach — critical for safe, effective AI interactions.*

- **Personal Trigger Mapping Framework** — Four components of a fully mapped trigger: (1) The situation/context (what was happening externally), (2) The thought or interpretation (what the mind made it mean), (3) The body sensation (where the feeling lives physically), (4) The behavioral response (what I did or wanted to do). Mapping all four gives the person agency that naming the feeling alone doesn't. *(Semantic Search)*

- **Trigger Journaling Practice** — Daily or near-daily log of: What happened? What did I think? What did I feel in my body? What did I do? Over time, this surfaces patterns that aren't visible in individual instances. *(Semantic Search)*

- **"Name It to Tame It" Principle** — When an emotion is verbally labeled, brain activity in the amygdala decreases and prefrontal cortex activity increases — shifting from reactive to reflective. The more specific the label (not "upset" but "humiliated"), the more effectively this neural shift occurs. *(The Whole-Brain Child)*

- **Suppressing Feelings Amplifies Them** — Suppression does not reduce the power of a feeling — it increases it. Suppressed emotions drive behavior invisibly; named emotions give a choice. The fear that awareness will make feelings more overwhelming is the opposite of the truth. *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*

- **Emotions vs. Moods Distinction** — An emotion is triggered by a specific situation and comes and goes with circumstances. A mood is a longer-lasting general state that acts as a container holding multiple emotions. Recognizing the difference allows for more accurate labeling and more effective regulation choices. *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*

- **Passive vs. Active Coping** — Passive coping (scrolling, substances, gaming to escape, excessive venting) temporarily reduces emotional discomfort but makes it stronger on the rebound because it denies the chance to actually process and manage the distress. Active tools (labeling, breathwork, movement, journaling, nature) are harder in the moment but build real capacity. *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*

- **Anxiety and Excitement as Identical** — The physical sensations of anxiety and excitement are neurologically identical (racing heart, stomach churning, jitteriness). The difference is interpretation. Choosing to label these sensations as excitement — and directing attention toward the positives in the situation — is a legitimate reappraisal technique, not denial. *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*

- **Sensory Triggers as Emotional Memory Portals** — For autistic individuals and others with sensory sensitivities, sensory triggers can be emotional memory portals — a particular sound, texture, or smell can immediately access an emotional state with unusual intensity. Understanding which sensory inputs are calming and which are activating is essential profile data. *(Semantic Search)*

- **IFS — Parts, Exiles, and Protectors** — The Internal Family Systems model: emotional reactions often come from "parts" — internal sub-personalities. Exiles carry painful memories or emotions; protectors keep exiles out of consciousness, sometimes in extreme ways. The "Self" (curiosity, compassion, confidence, clarity) can witness parts without being overwhelmed by them. The shift from being flooded by a part to witnessing it with the Self's 8 C-qualities (curiosity, confidence, compassion, creativity, clarity, connectedness, courage, calmness) is the fundamental IFS move. *(You Are the One — IFS/Schwartz)*

- **The "Whoosh" Phenomenon** — The sudden flooding sensation when an exile becomes activated (triggered). The protector's job is to prevent this; the exile wants to be seen. Learning to recognize "I just got whooshed" is the beginning of working with the experience rather than being controlled by it. *(You Are the One — IFS/Schwartz)*

- **Speaking For vs. From Parts** — A critical distinction in emotional honesty: "I notice I feel hurt" (speaking for the part — the Self witnessing the part) vs. "You're being terrible to me" (speaking from the part — the part running the show). The first creates connection; the second creates conflict. *(You Are the One — IFS/Schwartz)*

- **Prefrontal Cortex Maturation in Teens** — The prefrontal cortex (impulse control, emotional regulation) is still developing through the mid-twenties. This means teens are biologically more prone to intense emotional reactions than adults — not because of character failure, but because of developmental reality. This reframe removes shame from the equation. *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*

- **4-7-8 Breathing Technique** — Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 7, exhale for 8. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and is one of the most portable and reliable self-soothing tools for any age. *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*

## Section 8: Self-Knowledge for Children — Age-Specific Patterns

*Critical section for MyAIM Family. Children need fundamentally different discovery approaches at different developmental stages. This is InnerWorkings' most complex adaptation challenge.*

- **Discovery Phase vs. Scholar Phase** — Children before age ~11-12 are in the Discovery Phase: exploring the world broadly, building neural capacity through play and variety, not ready for self-directed specialized development. The Scholar Phase begins in adolescence: capable of self-directed learning, setting goals, examining identity consciously. InnerWorkings interface and LiLa's questions must be calibrated to this phase distinction. *(TJEd philosophy)*

- **Children as Young as 5 Can Be Self-Aware** — Children can be remarkably self-aware about their own feelings, preferences, and reactions when given appropriate language and space. The mistake is adult projection ("you must be feeling X") rather than curious inquiry ("what does that feel like for you?"). *(Raising Human Beings — Greene)*

- **"Kids Do Well If They Can" Reframe** — When children are not meeting expectations, the first hypothesis should always be a capability gap, not a motivation gap. Asking "what's preventing them from succeeding?" rather than "how do we make them try harder?" leads to collaborative problem-solving instead of punitive response. *(Raising Human Beings — Greene)*

- **Behavior as Signal, Not Identity** — Challenging behaviors are by-products of unsolved problems, not character defects or deliberate defiance. This reframe is the foundation for collaborative self-knowledge discovery with children. Telling a child their behavior reveals something bad about who they are closes off self-discovery; treating it as information to be curious about opens it. *(Raising Human Beings — Greene)*

- **Incompatibility as Developmental Feature** — Natural tension between a child's inherent characteristics and external expectations (school, family, peers) is not a problem to eliminate — it is a catalyst for growth and resilience when approached with understanding. The InnerWorkings profile helps identify where genuine incompatibility exists, so it can be worked with rather than fought against. *(Raising Human Beings — Greene)*

- **Self-Realization as Children's Fundamental Journey** — Every child's core developmental task is understanding and embracing their unique identity — skills, preferences, beliefs, values. Parents have a parallel task: recognizing and supporting that individuality rather than imposing their own vision. The InnerWorkings profile for a child belongs to the child, not the parent. *(Raising Human Beings — Greene)*

- **Age of Accountability (~8 Years)** — Around age 8, children develop the cognitive and moral capacity to understand consequences of their choices and begin to reflect on character. Before this age, self-knowledge conversations are best focused on feelings, preferences, and strengths — not character or values. *(TJEd philosophy)*

- **Love Language Expression by Development Stage** — Children express and receive love languages differently at different ages: Physical Touch is primary for infants and toddlers regardless of later language. Words of Affirmation require sufficient language development. Quality Time and Gifts are accessible across all ages. Acts of Service is one of the last to differentiate clearly. Never assume chronological age maps to developmental stage. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*

- **Upstairs/Downstairs Brain** — Downstairs brain (brainstem, limbic system) handles survival instincts and big emotions — fully developed at birth. Upstairs brain (prefrontal cortex) handles nuanced decision-making, emotional regulation, empathy, and moral thinking — still under construction through the mid-twenties. "Flipping the lid" = upstairs going offline when overwhelmed. You cannot reason with a flipped lid — must regulate first, then explain. *(The Whole-Brain Child)*

- **Connect Then Redirect** — Before any teaching, problem-solving, or correction, the first move is always emotional connection. "Connect and redirect" is the most effective sequence for children of any age: acknowledge the feeling (connect) before addressing the behavior (redirect). The connected child is neurologically capable of receiving guidance; the unconnected child is not. *(The Whole-Brain Child)*

- **Name It to Tame It with Children** — When a child is flooded with emotion, help them put the experience into words and narrative: "You were so angry when she took your toy." Narration of the experience (especially with younger children who can't narrate themselves) engages the left-brain language center and helps integrate the experience, reducing its power over the child. *(The Whole-Brain Child)*

- **Implicit vs. Explicit Memory** — Implicit memory stores emotional and bodily reactions below conscious awareness; explicit memory stores facts and narratives that can be consciously recalled. Many of a child's (and adult's) emotional reactions are driven by implicit memory — triggered without knowing why. Understanding this removes shame from "irrational" reactions. *(The Whole-Brain Child)*

- **Mindsight — Insight + Empathy** — The ability to see your own mind clearly (insight) and the minds of others accurately (empathy). This is the meta-skill that underlies all emotional intelligence. It can be deliberately developed through practice. *(The Whole-Brain Child)*

- **Love Languages for Special Needs Children — Behavioral Detection** — For nonverbal or minimally verbal children: observe what calms them (soothing language → Physical Touch or specific stimuli), what motivates them (reward patterns → revealing preferences), and where they voluntarily spend energy. The five-language framework applies — detection method adapts to the child's communication capacities. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*

- **Strength-Based Paradigm for Neurodivergent Children** — Every cognitive difference has a corresponding strength if the frame is right. ADHD brains are physically different, not defective — they have enhanced pattern recognition, crisis response, and divergent thinking capacity. Down syndrome individuals often have visual processing as a legitimate intelligence form that outperforms neurotypical peers. "Fish being asked to climb trees" is the metaphor for educational mismatch, not for limitation. *(Redefining the Reality of Down Syndrome; Semantic Search)*

- **Presuming Competence** — The default assumption for any child, including those with significant cognitive differences, should be competence rather than limitation. Performance demands and processing abilities are often mismatched, making a child appear less capable than they are. Giving the benefit of the doubt and removing barriers to expression typically reveals capacities that deficit-based approaches missed. *(Redefining the Reality of Down Syndrome)*

- **Attention as Foundation of All Cognition** — Before learning, memory, reasoning, or social skill development can occur, attention must be present. For children with attention differences (ADHD, autism, trauma history), building the InnerWorkings profile requires understanding what engages their attention — and designing discovery conversations around those entry points, not standard formats. *(Redefining the Reality of Down Syndrome)*

- **Visual Processing as Legitimate Intelligence** — Educational systems that accommodate visual learning styles see dramatic improvements in engagement and achievement among students with cognitive differences (including Down syndrome and dyslexia). Visual intelligence is not a consolation prize — it is a genuine form of knowing. *(Redefining the Reality of Down Syndrome)*

## Section 9: Guided Discovery Questions for LiLa

*The most valuable section for InnerWorkings implementation. These questions are designed to surface genuine self-knowledge through conversation — not surveys. Organized by category.*

### Personality & Core Identity
- "What is something you value so deeply that you would be uncomfortable if everyone abandoned it?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "What's an injustice or imperfection that you find genuinely hard to let go of — that bothers you long after others have moved on?" *(Semantic Search / Type 1)*
- "When you're doing something you love, do you prefer to have a clear destination first, or do you prefer to discover where it leads as you go?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "Do you tend to gather information and prepare extensively before acting, or do you prefer to learn by jumping in?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "What does your energy feel like at your best — focused and purposeful, or expansive and exploring?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "How do you respond when you realize you've made a significant mistake? What goes through your mind first?" *(Enneagram — Type 1)*
- "What does unconditional love look like to you — and do you believe you've experienced it?" *(Enneagram — Type 2)*
- "When you imagine your future, what do you most hope people say about you?" *(Enneagram — Type 3)*
- "What feeling or experience do you most long for, but rarely let yourself fully have?" *(Enneagram — Type 4)*
- "Do you find it easy or hard to ask for help? What does it cost you emotionally to need something from someone?" *(Enneagram — Type 5)*
- "Where in your life do you feel genuinely safe — and where do you feel like you're always bracing for something to go wrong?" *(Enneagram — Type 6)*
- "When is the last time you felt genuinely satisfied — not just done, but satisfied?" *(Enneagram — Type 7)*
- "What situations make you feel most alive? What situations make you want to take over and run things yourself?" *(Enneagram — Type 8)*
- "When conflict arises, is your first instinct to engage with it, withdraw from it, or smooth it over?" *(Enneagram — Type 9)*

### Strengths & Talents
- "What feels like play to you but looks like work to everyone else?" *(StrengthsFinder 2.0)*
- "What is something you learned unusually quickly — that others seemed to struggle with much more than you?" *(StrengthsFinder 2.0)*
- "When have you been in a state of total absorption — losing track of time because you were so engaged? What were you doing?" *(StrengthsFinder 2.0 / Flow)*
- "What do others regularly come to you for help with — where they seem to trust your judgment more than their own?" *(StrengthsFinder 2.0)*
- "What kind of recognition or acknowledgment feels most meaningful to you?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "Is there something you find yourself doing naturally that surprises people — where their reaction is 'how did you do that?'" *(StrengthsFinder 2.0)*

### Processing Style
- "When you need to figure out a complicated problem, what does your natural process look like?" *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*
- "Do you typically need time alone after social interaction to restore your energy, or does social interaction energize you?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "When you learn something new, do you prefer to read about it first, see it demonstrated, talk through it with someone, or just try it yourself?" *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*
- "When you're in a conversation and you hear a new idea, do you tend to immediately engage with it, or do you need time to process it before you can respond well?" *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*
- "How do you know when your thinking is done and you're ready to make a decision?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "What drains your energy most reliably — busy social environments, repetitive tasks, open-ended ambiguity, or high-stakes deadlines?" *(Semantic Search)*

### Love Languages (Adults)
- "What does your partner do that makes you feel most loved? What do they do that hurts most?" *(5 Love Languages — Chapman)*
- "When you want to show someone you care, what do you naturally do first?" *(5 Love Languages — Chapman)*
- "If you could choose one thing your partner would do consistently to make you feel cherished, what would it be?" *(5 Love Languages — Chapman)*
- "What expression of love from someone important to you has stayed with you — that you still remember years later?" *(5 Love Languages — Chapman)*

### Love Languages (Children)
- "When [child] is upset, what is the thing that most reliably calms them?" *(5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*
- "What does [child] most frequently ask for from you?" *(5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*
- "What gift, activity, or kind of time together has produced the highest emotional response in them?" *(5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*
- "How does [child] show love to you? What do they do when they want to demonstrate care?" *(5 Love Languages of Children — Chapman)*

### Values & Faith
- "What principle would you refuse to violate even if it cost you something important?" *(7 Habits — Covey)*
- "When you look at how you actually spend your time and energy (not how you intend to), what does it reveal about what you truly value?" *(7 Habits)*
- "What would feel like a meaningful contribution with your life — that isn't about personal success or comfort?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "How does your faith or spiritual life show up in everyday decisions? Or does it — and if not, does that gap bother you?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "When you're in a season of doubt or spiritual distance, what is the thing that tends to draw you back?" *(Semantic Search)*

### Emotional Triggers & Patterns
- "What kind of comment, situation, or event tends to trigger a reaction in you that feels disproportionate to what happened?" *(IFS/Schwartz)*
- "Where in your body do you first notice you're becoming stressed or upset? What does that feel like physically?" *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*
- "Is there a type of person or interaction pattern that consistently drains you or puts you on edge, even when nothing overtly bad has happened?" *(IFS/Schwartz)*
- "When you get emotionally flooded, what do you most need from the people around you?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "After a really difficult conversation or conflict, how long does it typically take before you feel back to baseline? What helps that process?" *(Semantic Search)*

### Self-Awareness Exercises (Teen-Specific)
- "If you had to name the feeling you've been carrying most this week — what would it be? (It's okay if you're not sure — start with unsure and we can work from there.)" *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*
- "What's a belief about yourself that you've had for a long time? Where do you think it came from?" *(Growth Mindset for Teens)*
- "Think about the last time you failed at something. What story did you tell yourself about what it meant about you?" *(Growth Mindset for Teens)*
- "What's something you can't do yet — but actually want to?" *(Growth Mindset for Teens)*
- "What would you do with your time if no one was watching and there were no expectations?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "When you compare yourself to your peers, what do you most often feel — behind, ahead, or just different? What do you make of that?" *(The Emotionally Intelligent Teen)*

### Age-Appropriate for Children (ages 5-11, Discovery Phase)
- "What's your very favorite thing to do? What do you love about it?" *(TJEd)*
- "When you feel sad or scared, what is the thing that helps you feel better?" *(5 Love Languages of Children)*

- "What is one thing that makes you, you? Something special about you that no one else has quite the same way?" *(Semantic Search)*
- "What is something that feels very hard for you — that you wish were easier?" *(Raising Human Beings)*
- "What do you love doing with [parent/sibling] more than anything else?" *(5 Love Languages of Children)*

## Key Design Principles (Distilled from All Sources)

1. **Self-knowledge is built, not found.** Profile data emerges through guided conversation, behavioral observation, and repeated reflection — not one-time surveys. LiLa should treat the profile as always deepening.

2. **Every family member gets their own profile.** Children's profiles are not subordinate to parents'. They belong to the child. LiLa's access and sharing behavior must reflect this.

3. **Neurodivergent-first design.** Every discovery path must work without standard verbal-reflective capacity. Visual indicators, behavioral observation data, and caregiver input are equally valid data sources.

4. **Never label growth areas as weaknesses.** This is a non-negotiable rule across all surfaces — UI labels, LiLa responses, and generated content.

5. **The profile is always provisional.** People change. Love languages shift in priority. Strengths mature. Mindsets grow. The InnerWorkings profile should always be understood as "current best understanding" not permanent label.

6. **Active engagement > passive input.** The most valuable profile data comes from LiLa conversations where the person is actively engaging with their experience, not from filling out forms.

7. **Behavioral signals are primary.** Especially for children and neurodivergent individuals, what a person does consistently is more reliable than what they say about themselves. The profile should be informed by both.

8. **Profile data feeds everything.** InnerWorkings is the foundation layer. Every other LiLa tool — Cyrano, Higgins, Safe Harbor, Optimizer, BigPlans, Family Dashboard — should be able to pull from this profile to provide personalized support.
