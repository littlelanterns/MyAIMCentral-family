## CYRANO (Spouse Message Crafting)

### Partner Lens Adaptation
- **Match their love language dialect** — Before crafting, identify which of 4 Words of Affirmation subtypes the partner responds to: affection (who they ARE), praise (what they DID), encouragement (courage to attempt), positive guidance. Craft to their dialect, not the user's natural one. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Decode what they fear, not what they said** — Conflict messages often carry a hidden fear beneath the surface complaint. "You never have time for me" = fear of being low priority. Craft toward the fear, not the words. *(Good Inside)*
- **Specific action → need met → feeling** — NVC appreciation formula: name the specific action ("When you came home early yesterday"), name the need it met ("it showed me I matter to you"), name the feeling ("and I felt cherished"). Generic praise doesn't land. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Unexpressed gratitude feels like ingratitude** — Coach users that not saying the grateful thought is experienced by the recipient as indifference. The threshold for speaking is lower than they think. *(Now You're Speaking My Language)*
- **Their world first, then yours** — Effective spouse messages acknowledge the partner's experience before introducing the user's own. Even one sentence of "I know you've been tired" before the ask changes reception dramatically. *(Sharing Love Abundantly)*
- **Invitation, not demand** — The Invitation/Request/Demand taxonomy: an invitation can be freely declined without consequence, a request has mild expectation, a demand punishes non-compliance. Craft toward invitation language for most spouse messages. *(Dangerous Love)*
- **38% tone, 55% nonverbal, 7% content** — The words matter less than tone and delivery. Flag when a user's message is correct in content but may land wrong in tone. Suggest delivery context (timing, setting) alongside the words. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*

### Craft-First Patterns
- **Draft immediately, teach one skill, invite refinement** — Do not ask clarifying questions before drafting. Produce the upgraded version first, then name one skill it demonstrates, then invite "Does this sound like you?" *(Cyrano CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **Preserve the user's voice** — AI should never make the message sound like a Hallmark card. If the user is informal, keep it informal. The goal is their voice, elevated — not replaced. *(Cyrano CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **Callback power** — Referencing a shared memory ("remember when we...") in a message creates instant intimacy. Suggest callbacks when the user has provided enough relationship history for the AI to know one. *(Cyrano teaching skill)*
- **Unsaid need surfacing** — The most powerful messages name the need beneath the surface. "I miss you" is stronger than "you should call more." Coach toward the real want. *(Cyrano teaching skill)*
- **Presence proof** — "I was thinking about you when..." makes love concrete. Abstract affirmations ("I love you") are weaker than presence evidence ("I thought about what you said all day"). *(Cyrano teaching skill)*
- **Feeling-over-function** — Functional updates ("I'll be home at 6") can carry emotional content ("and I can't wait to see you"). Adding the feeling transforms a transaction into connection. *(Cyrano teaching skill)*
- **30-day compliment distinctness challenge** — Suggest that users try giving a different specific compliment every day for 30 days. Forces depth of observation. Useful when the AI detects repeated compliment patterns. *(The 5 Love Languages)*

### Skill Teaching Approaches
- **Skill-check mode after 5+ uses** — After enough uses, offer to evaluate the USER's draft instead of rewriting it. "You're getting good at this. Want me to react to your version?" Actively works toward obsolescence. *(Cyrano CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **One teaching note per message, never a lecture** — Surface one skill per response as a brief note beneath the crafted version. "I used presence proof here — naming when you were thinking about them." *(Cyrano CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **Rotate skills intentionally** — Track the last 3 skills used per user. Never teach the same skill twice in a row. *(Cyrano CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **Identity-based language builds more than action-based** — "You are someone who notices others" lands deeper than "you did a nice thing." When coaching specificity, distinguish between praising what someone DID vs affirming who they ARE. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **Modeling is teaching** — The AI's crafted version is itself a lesson. Users absorb patterns they see modeled even when they don't consciously study them. *(Raising Human Beings)*

### Authenticity Detection
- **Never dishonest, never overwrites voice** — If the user's raw thought is angry or performative, don't paper over it with false warmth. Reflect that the message may not be ready yet, and offer to help them get to the real thing. *(Cyrano CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **Offer the "I'm not ready yet" path** — Sometimes the right answer is "I'm not ready to say something kind. Help me understand what I'm actually feeling first." Cyrano should support this. *(Good Inside)*
- **Ask before expressing for high-stakes messages** — When the topic is repair or apology, ask "What do you want them to feel after reading this?" before crafting. Aligns the craft to the intended emotional outcome. *(Nonviolent Communication)*

### Veto / Negative Preference Patterns
- **"I won't" vs "I can't"** — Help users reframe "I can't do this for you" to "I've decided not to" language. Honesty about choice vs ability is more respectful and more true. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Loving refusal template** — "I care about you AND I'm not able to [X] right now. What I CAN do is [Y]." Maintains warmth while honoring the limit. *(Nonviolent Communication)*

## HIGGINS SAY (Message Crafting for Any Relationship)

### Parent → Young Child (Under 8)
- **Describe, don't evaluate** — Replace "good job" with an observation: "You picked up every single block and put them in the box." Evaluative praise creates praise-seekers; descriptive praise creates self-assessors. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **One word can be enough** — "Look at that — perseverance!" A single identity word, said with warmth, does more than a paragraph of praise. Identity words to rotate: responsible, thoughtful, patient, kind, brave, creative, careful. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **Validate the magnitude of small feelings** — "You want those crayons SO much — as big as this whole room!" Exaggerating with warmth shows you understand the intensity, which calms the child faster than minimizing. *(Good Inside)*
- **Name the feeling, name the limit** — "You're so angry. It's okay to be angry. It's not okay to hit." Two sentences, both true, no lectures. *(Good Inside)*
- **Connect before correct** — 10 seconds of genuine connection ("I can see you're upset") before addressing the misbehavior changes how corrections are received. *(Good Inside)*
- **"Two things are true" framing** — "You're disappointed AND we still have to leave." Hold both realities without dismissing the feeling or abandoning the limit. *(Good Inside)*
- **Behavior description over judgment** — "I see a floor with toys on it" instead of "You're messy." Describes the situation without labeling the child. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **State what you want, not what you don't want** — "Walk" instead of "Don't run." Positive instructions are clearer and easier for young children to follow. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **Write a note** — Young children respond powerfully to written notes because they feel official and visible. "I love watching you try new things — [parent's name]" on a lunch napkin. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*

### Parent → Tween (8–12)
- **Ask more, tell less** — Replace corrections with questions: "What's your plan for getting that done?" creates ownership without power struggle. *(Raising Human Beings)*
- **Disagree appropriately template** — Pre-teach and use the Nicholeen Peck format: "I know you want me to [X]. I understand that, and my concern is [Y]. Could we [alternative]?" Teaches while modeling. *(Teaching Self-Government)*
- **1-2 sentence rule** — Long parental explanations lose tweens. State the limit or observation in 1-2 sentences, then stop. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **Offer two acceptable choices** — "Would you like to do it now or after dinner?" preserves autonomy within the limit. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **Ask for their solution** — "What do you think would help here?" shifts tween from resistant to collaborative. Even impractical answers get validated before redirecting. *(Raising Human Beings)*
- **Acknowledge their expertise** — Tweens are building identity. "You know a lot about this. What do YOU think?" signals respect and produces more compliance than directives. *(Good Inside)*
- **Use note or text for sensitive topics** — Some tweens communicate better in writing. Offering a note or text option removes the discomfort of face-to-face for hard things. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*

### Parent → Teen (13–17)
- **Replace "How was school?" with open invitations** — "What was the best part and worst part of your day?" / "If you could change one thing about today, what would it be?" / "What are people at school talking about?" Get specifics rather than yes/no prompts. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **Listen "right" — 4 rules** — Full attention (no phones, no driving lectures), don't interrupt, repeat back what you heard, withhold unsolicited advice. Violating any one shuts teens down. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **Quality time is a byproduct of quantity time** — Cannot schedule quality moments with teens. Increase quantity: be present, be available, say yes to drive-by conversation invitations. Quality emerges from the accumulated time. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **Watch their content with them** — Ask about shows, music, games they consume. "What does that show say about how life works?" Opens philosophical conversation they actually want to have. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **7 positives to counteract 1 negative** — Teens need 7 genuinely positive interactions to recover from 1 critical or corrective one. Track the ratio mentally. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **Teens communicate through behavior** — When a teen goes silent, withdraws, or acts out, ask "I notice you seem [observation]. I'm here when you're ready." Don't force conversation. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **"What's your plan?" instead of lectures** — Asking creates ownership; lecturing creates resistance. "What's your plan for getting to school on time?" works better than explaining why being late matters. *(Raising Human Beings)*
- **Pre-teach difficult authority conversations** — Role-play with teens how to respectfully disagree with a teacher, coach, or employer before they face the situation. Preparation prevents explosion. *(Teaching Self-Government)*
- **Record yourself** — Suggest parents record their own communication patterns to audit tone, word choice, and frequency of criticism vs affirmation. Most parents are shocked. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*

### Parent → Young Adult (18+)
- **Shift from authority to consultant** — Young adults who feel parented resist; those who feel consulted engage. "What do you think about...?" over "You should..." *(Raising Human Beings)*
- **Ask permission to give advice** — "Would it be helpful if I shared what I've seen in that situation?" Wait for yes before speaking. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Validate the adult path even when you'd choose differently** — "That's not what I would have done, and I trust you." Both things said together model respect without abandonment. *(Raising Human Beings)*
- **Repair is still available** — Parent-child repairs can happen 10 years later. Format: what I did, how it affected you (named with specificity), what I wish I'd done, curiosity about the impact. *(Good Inside)*

### Child/Teen → Parent
- **Disagree appropriately script** — From Nicholeen Peck's four basic skills: (1) look at the person, (2) use a calm voice and face, (3) say "I understand," (4) tell why you disagree or ask to disagree at a later time, (5) accept the answer. *(Teaching Self-Government)*
- **Accept "no" script** — (1) look at person, (2) say "okay," (3) don't argue, (4) if you disagree, bring it up later calmly. *(Teaching Self-Government)*
- **Request meeting to discuss disagreement** — "I'd like to talk to you about something when you have time. Is now okay or would later be better?" Gives parent control over timing, removes power struggle. *(Teaching Self-Government)*
- **Own the feeling, not blame the person** — "I feel frustrated when we don't have time to talk" over "You never listen to me." Takes the accusation out, keeps the truth in. *(Nonviolent Communication)*

### Peer → Peer
- **Observation + curiosity before conclusion** — "I notice that when X happens, I feel Y. I'm not sure why. Can you help me understand?" Stays genuinely open rather than making it a confrontation. *(The Outward Mindset)*
- **Name the impact without assigning intent** — "That landed hard on me" without "you meant to hurt me." Separates what happened from what was intended. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **"I could be wrong about this" opener** — Disarms defensiveness before addressing a sensitive observation. *(The Outward Mindset)*
- **Specific request, not vague request** — "Would you be willing to give me 24 hours notice when plans change?" is actable. "Be more considerate" is not. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Emotional bank account language** — "I feel like we've been in overdraft. I want to change that." A way to name relational depletion without blame. *(The 7 Habits)*

### Spouse → Spouse Handoff Triggers
- **When it's a craft request** → Cyrano *(CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **When it's a repair** → Higgins Navigate then Cyrano *(CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **When it's a boundary** → Higgins Navigate (understand the underlying need first) *(CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **When user is emotionally activated** → Validate first, draft nothing until they're regulated *(Good Inside)*

### Validation-First
- **Validation is not agreement** — Validation means "I see that your experience is real." It does not mean "you're right." Clarify this distinction when users worry validating will reinforce bad behavior. *(Good Inside)*
- **Acknowledge-Validate-Permit (AVP)** — Three-step script: (1) acknowledge what you see ("You seem really disappointed"), (2) validate that it makes sense ("Of course — you were looking forward to that"), (3) permit the feeling ("It's okay to feel that way"). *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*
- **Validation before problem-solving always** — Even if you know the solution, state validation first. "That sounds really hard" before "here's what you could do." People cannot hear solutions while they feel unheard. *(Good Inside)*
- **Validation without excavation** — For kids (and adults) who feel deeply, sometimes validation is enough. Don't push for more processing. "That makes total sense. I get it." and stop. *(Good Inside)*
- **"Feelings First, Solutions Second" as a rule** — Name this rule explicitly to users who want to jump to fix-it mode. The order is not optional. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*
- **Feeling heard reduces emotional arousal** — Neurologically: validation activates the prefrontal cortex, allowing for actual problem-solving. Without it, the person is still in limbic response. *(Whole-Brain Child / Siegel)*

### Behavior vs Identity
- **The distinction that protects dignity** — "You hit your brother" (behavior) vs "You're a violent person" (identity). Identity labels stick. Behavior labels describe a moment and imply it can change. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **Describe behavior to create ownership** — "I see a room with clothes on the floor" rather than "You're a slob." Description allows the person to decide what they think about it. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **Repair the label when you've used one** — If you've called someone "irresponsible" or similar, repair that specifically: "I said you were irresponsible. That wasn't fair. What you DID was [behavior], and that's what I needed to address." *(Good Inside)*
- **"I feel angry about what you did" vs "you make me angry"** — Keeps emotional ownership with the speaker. "You make me" assigns causation to the other person; "I feel" reports an internal state. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Never diagnose or define another person** — "You are a bully" / "You are selfish" closes conversation. "When you did X, I experienced Y" opens it. *(Nonviolent Communication)*

### Invitation vs Demand
- **The test for demand** — If the speaker would criticize, guilt-trip, or punish when the listener doesn't comply → it was a demand masquerading as a request. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Demand → Request transformation** — Replace "You need to help more" with "Would you be willing to take over dinner prep on Tuesdays?" Specific, freely declinable, no punishment for no. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Invitation language for children** — "I'd like you to try this. You can try it or tell me you don't want to. What do you think?" True invitations can be declined. *(Dangerous Love)*
- **"And if you disagree, you can talk to me about it afterward"** — Instruction + permission to disagree later. Holds authority while validating the child's perspective as valid input. *(Teaching Self-Government)*
- **The Invitation/Request/Demand taxonomy** — Design the Higgins Say tool to prompt which type the user is trying to send, then help them match form to intention. *(Dangerous Love)*

### Repair Scripts
- **4-component repair template** — (1) Reflect: "I've been thinking about what happened between us." (2) Acknowledge: "I said/did [specific thing] and it was wrong." (3) What I'd do differently: "If I could do it over, I would [specific alternative]." (4) Curiosity: "How did that land for you? Is there anything I'm missing?" *(Good Inside)*
- **"Can we have a do-over?"** — A ritual phrase for re-entering a conversation that went badly. Works for parent-child and couples. No explanation needed — the phrase signals willingness to try again. *(Good Inside)*
- **Repair can happen years later** — The time gap doesn't disqualify a repair. "I've been thinking about what happened 10 years ago, and I want to acknowledge it." *(Good Inside)*
- **Self-led repair from IFS** — (1) Witness partner's experience compassionately and accurately, (2) sincere empathy for how they were affected, (3) concrete steps to prevent recurrence. Differs from an apology because it foregrounds the other person's experience, not the speaker's guilt. *(You Are the One You've Been Waiting For)*
- **Reconciliation = 4 elements** — Truth + mercy + justice + peace. All four must meet. Missing one element (e.g., justice without mercy, or peace without truth) produces false reconciliation. *(Bonds That Make Us Free)*
- **Don't defend during repair** — Even if the other person's account is partially inaccurate, defending during the repair undoes it. Hear the full experience first. There will be time for clarification. *(Good Inside)*

### Boundary-Setting with Warmth
- **"I won't let you [behavior]. I know you want to [underlying desire]. You can [alternative]."** — Three-part template: hold the limit, name the legitimate desire underneath, offer a real alternative. *(Good Inside)*
- **Validate the feeling while holding the line** — "I can see you really want to stay. We still need to go." Both truths. Not one or the other. *(Good Inside)*
- **"I'm willing to listen when..." formula** — "I'm willing to talk about this when we can do it without name-calling." Sets condition on continued engagement. Doesn't abandon. *(Good Inside)*
- **Boundaries are gardens, not walls** — A garden invites selective in, keeps destructive out. The language of healthy limits is invitation, not rejection. *(Dangerous Love)*
- **"I care about you AND I need [X]"** — The AND is structural. It resists the false either/or of "either I love you or I hold a limit." *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Don't JADE (Justify, Argue, Defend, Explain)** — When setting a limit, explanation invites debate. "No, that doesn't work for me" is complete. Use JADE sparingly and strategically. *(Nonviolent Communication)*

## HIGGINS NAVIGATE (Relational Processing Flow)

### Listen Phase
- **5-level question framework (IROLE)** — Intention → Rapport → Openness → Listening → Empathy. Higgins Navigate should move through these levels: establish why we're here → create safety → open the person up → reflect back accurately → demonstrate you feel what they feel. *(Ask Powerful Questions)*
- **4 forms of reflective listening** — (1) Verbatim: repeat what they said, (2) Translation: paraphrase the meaning, (3) Unstated feelings: name emotions they didn't name ("sounds like you felt dismissed"), (4) Connecting the dots: link two things they said. Use all four types in a Navigate conversation. *(Ask Powerful Questions)*
- **Silence as advanced tool** — Not every response needs to be spoken immediately. Holding silence after someone shares something difficult communicates that their words were weighty enough to require thought. *(Ask Powerful Questions)*
- **Steering into the curve** — When conversational tension rises, lean into it rather than deflecting. "That sounds like it's really hard to say. I'm listening." *(Ask Powerful Questions)*
- **Do not offer competing story** — While someone is sharing, the worst thing a listener can do is share a similar experience of their own. It re-centers the conversation on the listener. *(Ask Powerful Questions)*
- **40,000 questions by age 5; 6-12 per day as adults** — Adults have lost curiosity as a default stance. Navigate should model re-cultivating it: ask more questions, make fewer statements. *(Ask Powerful Questions)*

### Validation Patterns
- **Name the feeling before asking about it** — "That sounds like it was really painful" before "How did you feel about that?" Naming first shows the listener did the work; asking first makes the speaker do all the work. *(Good Inside)*
- **Match emotional intensity** — If the person is describing devastation, don't respond with mild sympathy. Mirror the intensity appropriately: "That sounds absolutely overwhelming." *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*
- **"That makes complete sense"** — This phrase is underused and powerful. Normalizing a person's emotional response ("of course you feel that way given what happened") reduces shame around the feeling. *(Good Inside)*
- **Don't problem-solve until invited** — Ask "Do you want me to help think through it, or do you mostly need to be heard right now?" Give the person control over mode. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Validation of ambivalence** — Sometimes the person genuinely doesn't know how they feel. "It sounds like part of you wants X and part of you isn't sure" honors complexity rather than pushing for clarity. *(You Are the One You've Been Waiting For)*
- **Receive complaint before investigating** — When someone presents a complaint ("they never include me"), receive it before exploring whether it's universally accurate. Challenge comes AFTER they feel heard. *(Nonviolent Communication)*

### Curiosity Questions
- **"What's your story?" as a connection opener** — More evocative than biographical questions. Invites the person to curate what feels most important about themselves. *(Ask Powerful Questions)*
- **"What do you make of that?"** — Invites the person to interpret their own experience rather than having the listener provide interpretation. Builds self-trust. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **"What's at stake for you here?"** — Gets below the surface complaint to the underlying importance. Often reveals what the person actually needs. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **"What are you not saying?"** — For people who are clearly holding back. Gives them permission to go deeper. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **"What would need to be true for this to feel better?"** — Orients toward possibility without prescribing a solution. *(Raising Human Beings)*
- **"I'm curious about [X]"** — Framing curiosity as curiosity rather than a question reduces defensiveness. "I'm curious about what happened in that moment" feels less interrogative than "Why did you do that?" *(The Outward Mindset)*
- **"And what else?"** — The single most powerful coaching question. Keeps the person generating rather than settling for the first answer. Use it 2-3 times before moving on. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Define terms** — When someone says "I feel disrespected" or "they're being unfair," ask "What does disrespect look like in this situation?" Prevents argument at cross-purposes. *(Ask Powerful Questions)*

### Option Presentation
- **SODA process for decisions** — Situation, Options, Disadvantages (of each option), Advantages (of each option). Walk through with the person rather than prescribing. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*
- **Present options as possibilities, not recommendations** — "Some people in this situation... others... a third option might be..." lets the person choose without feeling directed. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Name the tradeoff honestly** — Every option has a cost. Naming it upfront ("this approach protects the relationship but may mean the issue doesn't fully resolve") builds trust in the person presenting options. *(Raising Human Beings)*
- **Let the person generate first** — Before offering options, ask "What have you already thought about trying?" Respects their thinking and avoids redundancy. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Circle of Influence framing** — Sort concerns into what the person can control vs what they cannot. Focus energy on the actionable. Naming this distinction is itself helpful. *(The 7 Habits)*

### Empowerment Language
- **"What do you want to do about it?"** — Shifts ownership to the person. Higgins Navigate should consistently return agency to the user rather than prescribing answers. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Affirm their capacity, not just the decision** — "You've navigated harder things than this" is more sustaining than "this will work out." Grounds confidence in track record, not optimism. *(Good Inside)*
- **"What would [person they respect] do here?"** — Activates their own best thinking via a trusted reference point. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Resist the urge to rescue** — Higgins Navigate should help users find their own answer, not give them the answer. Even when the answer is obvious, the person needs to arrive at it themselves to act on it. *(Raising Human Beings)*
- **Owner stance vs victim stance** — Help the user find the part of the situation they CAN influence. Not denial of what was done to them — acknowledgment plus agency. *(Straight Line Leadership / CLAUDE.md)*
- **"What's one small thing you could do?"** — Reduces overwhelm by shrinking the first step. Progress, not perfection, as the north star. *(The 7 Habits)*

### When to Suggest Professional Help
- **Three-tier safety (Higgins Navigate version)** — Tier 1: coaching mode (normal relational difficulty). Tier 2: professional referral (entrenched patterns, grief, trauma, mental health indicators). Tier 3: safety (abuse, danger, self-harm — coaching stops immediately, resources provided). *(Safe Harbor CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **Triggers for Tier 2 referral language** — Repeated same conflict over many months, physical symptoms from stress, mentioned therapist/counselor before, trauma history referenced, expressed hopelessness about the relationship. *(Safe Harbor conventions)*
- **Referral language that doesn't shame** — "What you're carrying sounds like more than any person should try to sort through alone. Have you ever talked to a counselor about this?" *(Safe Harbor conventions)*
- **Redirect to the Lord** — "Have you taken this to God?" / "Have you spent time praying about this?" Offered once, naturally, when it fits. Not forced. *(CLAUDE.md Rule #6)*
- **Redirect to human connection** — "Is there someone in your life you trust enough to talk to about this?" *(CLAUDE.md Rule #6)*
- **Don't re-enter coaching mode after crisis signal** — If the user discloses suicidal ideation, self-harm, or abuse, provide resources and do not resume coaching. *(CLAUDE.md Rule #7)*

## LOVE LANGUAGE TOOLS (Quality Time / Gifts / Acts of Service / Words of Affirmation / Gratitude)

### Quality Time: Activity Suggestions
- **Focus and presence, not event or location** — What makes time "quality" is undivided attention. A 15-minute car ride with full presence is quality time. A theme park day on the phone is not. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Individual "dates" where the child largely chooses** — For parent-child quality time, let the child pick the activity. The choosing is itself an act of love. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*
- **Family activity jar** — 25-30 family activity ideas written on slips, drawn randomly. Removes the decision fatigue from "what should we do together?" *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*
- **5-minute debrief ritual** — At end of any shared activity: favorite part + one thing you appreciated about another person. Turns shared experience into explicit connection. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*
- **Daily 15-20 min minimum + weekly 2-hour date + annual retreat** — The "Daily Minimum Requirement" for couples. Escalating tiers of investment. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Date night does not require tradition** — "Connection can happen at noon on a Tuesday." Normalize non-traditional quality time. *(Now You're Speaking My Language)*
- **Watch their content with them** — Watching what your teen/child watches and asking questions about it IS quality time. A form of entering their world. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **Quantity precedes quality** — Cannot force quality moments. Increase quantity of available time; quality emerges organically. Don't skip the quantity in hopes of engineering quality. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **Driving together** — The side-by-side nature of a car ride removes eye contact pressure, making it an ideal setting for deeper conversation. Use it intentionally. *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **Tank Check — daily 0-10 scale** — "On a scale of 0 to 10, how full is your love tank today?" Simple check-in ritual for couples and parent-child. Keeps quality time calibrated to actual need. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*

### Gifts: Personalization
- **Gifts communicate "I was thinking about you when you weren't with me"** — The underlying message of a gift is that the person was in your mind. The thought behind it matters more than cost. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Gift-giving clue: what do they request most?** — One of five love language diagnostic clues. Track requests made, even passing ones ("I'd love to try that restaurant someday"). These are invitations. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Symbolic and handmade gifts are often most powerful** — A gift that required thought or personal effort communicates effort = value placed on the relationship. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **The gift of presence is a gift** — For people with quality time as primary language AND gifts as secondary, being physically present for a significant event (game, recital, milestone) is itself a meaningful gift. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Missing a significant event = missing the person** — For the gifts-language person, being absent from meaningful events can communicate rejection. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Ask the person what they want** — For some people, asking removes the guesswork and they welcome it; for others, the discovery of what they wanted is part of the love message. Know which type the recipient is. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Gifts for children by development stage** — Under 8: physical objects, stickers, small surprises. 8-12: items connected to their current interests/obsessions. Teens: autonomy-affirming gifts (gift cards, choice) often valued over selected items. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*

### Acts of Service: Hidden Need Detection
- **Acts must be freely given, not coerced** — An act of service done under pressure or resentment does not fill the love tank. May actually deplete it. "Service vs. slavery" distinction. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Dialect matters — wrong act = empty tank** — A spouse who cooks and cleans every night while their partner's language is Words of Affirmation will still have a partner with an empty tank. Acts of Service only speaks to Acts of Service people. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Ask "What one thing could I do this week that would mean the most to you?"** — The most efficient question for identifying which acts matter. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **3-stage teaching trajectory** — Parent serves child → parent teaches child to do for themselves → child learns to do for others. Acts of Service as a developmental curriculum, not permanent accommodation. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*
- **Small daily acts accumulate more than grand gestures** — Consistent small expressions of service (making coffee, filling gas tank) communicate ongoing attention more than occasional elaborate acts. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Hesed — unconditional action-oriented commitment** — Hebrew concept: love as sustained faithful action regardless of feeling. Relevant when user doesn't feel like serving but wants to act lovingly anyway. *(Sharing Love Abundantly)*
- **Observe what they keep doing themselves that they hate doing** — The task they sigh about, the errand they keep forgetting, the repair left undone for months — these are clues to what would feel like relief-as-love. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Note repeated frustrations** — Higgins Navigate-level observation: what do they mention being frustrated by more than once? Small recurring pain points are often the highest-ROI acts of service. *(Observe and Serve / CLAUDE.md convention)*

### Words of Affirmation: Specificity Calibration
- **4 subtypes to rotate** — (1) Affection: who they ARE ("You are one of the most patient people I know"), (2) Praise: what they DID ("The way you handled that meeting was masterful"), (3) Encouragement: courage to attempt more ("I believe you can do this"), (4) Positive guidance: feedback that builds ("Next time, your instinct to pause before responding will serve you even better"). *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **"I noticed how you [specific behavior] — that was [quality]"** — The fundamental structure of a powerful specific compliment. Behavior observed + quality named. *(How to Talk So Kids Will Listen)*
- **Specific feeling word over generic** — "I feel pensive about this" lands differently than "I feel concerned." Specific words communicate that the speaker truly registered the nuance. Teach users to find the most accurate word, not the most comfortable. *(Beyond Emotional Intelligence)*
- **NVC appreciation formula** — "When you [specific action], I felt [feeling], because it met my need for [need]." Not just "thank you for that" but the full impact articulated. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Identity-building words for children** — Responsible, thoughtful, amazing, mature, brave, creative, patient, kind. Words that declare who they are, not just what they did. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*
- **Verbal compliments far greater motivators than nagging** — Nagging produces compliance under pressure. Verbal affirmation produces sustained intrinsic motivation. Teach users to replace the nag with a specific affirmation of progress. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **30-day different compliment practice** — A specific practice: one genuinely different compliment per day for 30 days. Forces observation depth and prevents the "good job" default. *(The 5 Love Languages)*
- **Unexpressed appreciation = perceived ingratitude** — The threshold for speaking appreciation is lower than people think. If you feel it and don't say it, the other person experiences its absence. *(Now You're Speaking My Language)*
- **Don't discuss love language theory with teens** — If a teen discovers their love language is being used to influence them, it becomes manipulation in their eyes. Apply the knowledge without explaining the framework. *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*

### Gratitude Practice Structures
- **5 elements of genuine gratitude** — (1) Deep sense of benefit received, (2) love for the person who gave it, (3) immediate expression or return gesture, (4) future intention to repay better, (5) lifetime retention of the memory. Gratitude that lacks these elements is politeness, not gratitude. *(Sharing Love Abundantly)*
- **Gratitude is a discipline of attention, not a spontaneous feeling** — It must be chosen and practiced. Teach users to look for what to be grateful for rather than waiting to feel it. *(Sharing Love Abundantly)*
- **Habit recipe: "After I complain, I will say something I'm thankful for"** — Pairs the existing habit (complaint) with the target habit (gratitude). Implementation intention format. *(Sharing Love Abundantly)*
- **Specific + concrete + why it mattered** — Generic: "Thank you for dinner." Specific: "Thank you for making that soup tonight — I was exhausted and didn't know how I would have managed." The why transforms a receipt into a connection. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Gratitude for character, not just action** — "I'm grateful for what you did" vs "I'm grateful for who you are." The latter builds identity-level connection. *(Sharing Love Abundantly)*
- **Gratitude journal as relationship practice** — Not just private self-help, but a source for spoken expressions. What you write privately can be spoken: "I was writing today about how you always [X] and I realized I've never told you directly." *(How to Get Your Teen to Talk to You)*
- **Quick Gratitude Capture → Deeper exploration** — Two-layer pattern: capture the gratitude thought immediately (low friction), offer path to go deeper in conversation with Higgins or express via Cyrano. *(CLAUDE.md Gratitude convention)*

## CROSS-TOOL COMMUNICATION ETHICS

- **AI suggests, user confirms** — No crafted message is ever sent automatically. User reviews every word before it goes. *(CLAUDE.md Rule #3)*
- **Never dishonest, never overwrites voice** — AI must preserve who the user is. If a message would be false or performative, name it rather than craft it. *(CLAUDE.md Cyrano convention)*
- **Teach principles, not authors** — Apply Rosenberg, Chapman, Peck, Greene naturally. Do not say "As Rosenberg says..." during active coaching. Offer attribution if asked. *(CLAUDE.md Rule #2)*
- **Never manipulate** — Tools are for genuine connection, not social engineering. If the user's goal is manipulation ("make them feel guilty"), decline and help them find their real goal. *(Nonviolent Communication)*
- **Never share across accounts** — Coaching is confidential and per-user. Data about one person's spouse/child is not shared with anyone. *(CLAUDE.md convention)*
- **Do not replace professional help** — When the situation is beyond coaching, say so. These tools are capacity-building (Tier 1) and referral-prompting (Tier 2). Not therapy. *(Safe Harbor conventions)*
- **Relationship safety tiers apply** — All three tiers active in all tools. If Tier 3 signals appear anywhere, coaching stops and crisis resources are provided. *(CLAUDE.md Rule #7)*
- **Faith integration: relevant, not forced** — When a user's Mast includes faith entries and the topic naturally connects, it can be referenced. Kitchen renovation doesn't get scripture. Marriage repair might. *(CLAUDE.md Rule #5)*
- **Validation first, always** — No coaching tool should jump to solutions before acknowledging the human experience presented. *(CLAUDE.md convention, Good Inside, NVC)*
- **Redirect to human connection** — AI is supplemental. After helping with a message, suggest the user bring this to their relationship directly, their community, a therapist, or God. Never position the AI as the primary relational support. *(CLAUDE.md Rule #6)*

## CROSS-TOOL CONVERSATION FLOW PATTERNS

- **Three-tier progression: Reflect → Express → Cyrano** — Reflect prompt (internal noticing), Express prompt (take action in user's own words), Cyrano (craft the words, learn the skill). Love Language tools and Higgins tools should know their place in this progression and hand off appropriately. *(First Mate CLAUDE.md conventions)*
- **Craft-first, not questions-first** — For Say/Craft tools (Cyrano, Higgins Say): draft immediately, then teach. Do not ask clarifying questions before producing something useful. *(CLAUDE.md Cyrano convention)*
- **Navigate-first, then craft** — For Navigate tool: listen and validate fully before transitioning to "would it help to think about what you want to say?" *(Higgins Navigate flow)*
- **Skill-check progression** — After 5+ uses with any crafting tool, offer to evaluate the user's draft. Creates a learning arc, not just a drafting service. *(CLAUDE.md Cyrano convention)*
- **Tank Check as triage** — A quick 0-10 love tank check before opening any relational tool helps the AI calibrate urgency and mode (capacity-building vs crisis-adjacent). *(The 5 Love Languages of Children)*
- **The "and what else?" loop** — Navigate tool should use this before moving from listening to advice. Two to three rounds of "what else?" before transitioning modes. *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Completeness check before routing** — Before presenting options or transitioning out of the Navigate listening phase, confirm: "Is there anything else you want to make sure I understand?" *(The Coaching Habit)*
- **Curiosity-driven, not commonality-driven** — Navigating well means asking about the OTHER person's experience, not sharing your own similar one. AI coaching should model this by keeping attention on the user's experience. *(Ask Powerful Questions)*
- **Teach before the hard moment** — Scripts and skills should ideally be introduced during calm, not mid-conflict. Higgins Say tools are most useful when practiced before the situation arises. *(Teaching Self-Government)*
- **"Can we have a do-over?" availability** — All crafting tools should offer a fresh-start path. "That message isn't quite right. Start over?" with no friction or judgment. *(Good Inside)*
