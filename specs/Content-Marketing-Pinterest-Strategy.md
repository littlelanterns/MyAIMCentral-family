# MyAIM Family: Content Marketing & Pinterest Strategy

**Purpose:** Outline for building a Pinterest/blog/article content pipeline that drives discovery among homeschool and ESA families, establishes MyAIM as the go-to educational software brand, and can eventually be taught in the AI Vault as a skill.

**Philosophy:** "I Go First" — we build and use the pipeline ourselves, document how we did it, then teach it.

---

## The Strategy in a Nutshell

Homeschool moms live on Pinterest. They search for curriculum ideas, weekly planners, organizational tips, and compliance help. They also search for disability resources, IEP templates, and special needs support. Every article we write becomes a Pinterest pin, every pin links to a blog post, every blog post positions MyAIM as the answer to the problem the article describes. The articles are genuinely helpful whether or not someone subscribes — but subscribers get the effortless version.

**Content → Pinterest Pin → Blog Post → "MyAIM does this for you" CTA → Subscriber**

The articles should be warm, practical, and written in the voice of The Lanterns We Live By — honest, no-BS, celebratory, from a mom who lives it. Not corporate marketing. Not clickbait. Real help from someone who understands the chaos.

---

## Content Pipeline (Build It, Then Teach It)

1. **Research:** Use AI to identify what homeschool/ESA moms are searching for (Pinterest trends, Google keyword tools)
2. **Write:** AI-assisted article drafting with founder voice editing (Claude + Tenise's review)
3. **Design:** Canva or Midjourney for Pinterest pin graphics (this is where a teen could own the pipeline)
4. **Schedule:** Tailwind or Pinterest native scheduler for consistent pinning
5. **Track:** Which pins drive traffic, which articles convert, which states respond
6. **Teach:** Document the whole workflow as an AI Vault tutorial series for Creator tier families

## Content Quality Standard: "They Get Me, and That Was Actually Useful"

Every article must pass two tests before it publishes:

**Test 1: "They get me."** The mom reading this should feel *seen* — not marketed to, not talked down to, not generically addressed. This means:
- Open with a scenario she's living right now, described so specifically she thinks "how do they know my Tuesday?"
- Use her language, not tech language. She doesn't have "workflow inefficiencies" — she has a kid who forgot his lunch and a dog who needs shots and a thing due Thursday she just remembered.
- Acknowledge the mess without judging it. She's not doing it wrong. She's doing an impossible job. We see that.
- Write from inside the experience, not above it. The founder IS this mom — that voice should come through in every piece. Not "here's what experts recommend" but "here's what I do at 9pm after the kids are finally down."

**Test 2: "That was actually useful."** The mom should walk away with something she can DO — today, for free, whether or not she ever subscribes. This means:
- Every article includes at least one actionable takeaway she can implement immediately without MyAIM. A real tip, a real framework, a real template, a real prompt she can paste into ChatGPT right now.
- The article solves a real problem, not a manufactured one. If we have to convince her she has the problem, it's the wrong article.
- MyAIM is positioned as "the effortless version of what we just taught you to do manually" — not as the only way. She should think "wow, that free tip helped... and if the app does it automatically, that's even better."
- No bait-and-switch. If the title promises "how to track 180 days without a spreadsheet," the article actually shows her how — even if the last section mentions that MyAIM does it automatically.

**The underlying philosophy (from Lantern Eight: Every Mom Counts):**
A mom who reads one article and never subscribes is still part of the mission. She got value. She might tell a friend. She might come back in six months when her situation changes. We don't measure her worth by whether she converted. The content IS the product — the app is just the product that does it for you.

**Voice check:** Read every article draft aloud and ask — "Would I send this to my best friend who's struggling, or would I be embarrassed?" If it sounds like marketing, rewrite it until it sounds like a real conversation between two moms who actually know each other.

### Future Avatar to Develop: The Sports Mom

**Quick sketch (to be fleshed out in a future session):**

The Sports Mom is managing a unique kind of chaos — not just household logistics but layered schedules that change weekly (game days, practice schedules, tournament weekends, carpools, team snack rotations), equipment tracking, multiple kids in overlapping seasons, volunteer coordination, and the emotional labor of being present at games while still keeping the rest of life running.

She might not identify as "tech-savvy" or "AI-curious" — she identifies as *busy*. Her phone is full of group texts from four different team parent chains. She's got a shared Google Sheet for the carpool rotation that three people keep breaking. She signed up for TeamSnap and it does one thing but not the other ten things she needs.

**What she'd need from MyAIM:** Calendar that handles recurring but irregular schedules (games that change weekly). Task templates deployable per kid per season. Carpool coordination. Equipment checklists. The ability to see all four kids' commitments in one view (Mom's Overview). And maybe most importantly — the Family Life Feed where she captures those game-day moments that fly by too fast.

**Article #21 above is written for her** — but a full avatar portrait would help us write more content targeted to this audience. Worth developing alongside the content pipeline.

## Pinterest Pin Strategy

**Pin types that work for homeschool moms:**
- Infographics ("5 Steps to Effortless Homeschool Reporting")
- Checklists ("ESA Spending Checklist: What Qualifies?")
- Before/after ("My homeschool tracking before MyAIM vs. after")
- Quote pins from The Lanterns We Live By ("AI doesn't do the important stuff — that's where your magic shines.")
- Step-by-step tutorials (screenshot walkthrough style)
- "Did you know?" facts ("Did you know Arizona ESA gives families $7,000+ per student for educational tools?")

**Pin design notes:**
- Use brand colors from the brandboard (#5A4033, #68A395, #D4E3D9, #D69A84, #2C5D60)
- Warm, organic aesthetic — not corporate tech
- Canva templates that a teen could replicate consistently
- Vertical format (2:3 ratio — 1000x1500px is the Pinterest sweet spot)
- Text overlay should be readable on mobile

- "Homeschool Organization Tips"
